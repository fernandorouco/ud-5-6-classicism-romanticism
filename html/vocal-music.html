From Classicism to Romanticism

Vocal Music

Opera's prime

Opera

Vocal music loses importance compared to instrumental music, but it continues to play an important role. Opera becomes one of the major genres of the period, with Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart standing out as one of its most important composers. The opera buffa is especially significant thanks to Mozart’s works. During this period, Italy stops being the only operatic reference in Europe. As a result, librettos are no longer written only in Italian, but also in vernacular languages. Therefore, the following types of opera emerge:

  • Singspiel: Singspiel: sung in German, which combines sung numbers with spoken dialogue  (Listening -> The Magic Flute by Mozart , The Queen of the Night’s aria)
  • French opéra-comique: which includes spoken dialogue and lighter musical content. (Listening -> Carmen by Georges Bizet, Aria - Habanera).

Opera seria in the Classical period was a genre that, although dominant, entered crisis due to its excessive virtuosity and its distance from dramatic action. This situation led to its reform by Christoph Willibald Gluck, who sought (buscar) to return opera to its dramatic essence. Gluck prioritised dramatic action and the text, deleting unnecessary ornaments and excessive vocal display. He also gave greater importance to the chorus and the orchestra, integrating them into the drama instead of using them as simple accompaniment. With this reform, Gluck aimed for clarity, balance and rationality, values closely linked to the ideals of the Enlightenment. (Listening -> Orfeo ed Eurídice by C.W. Gluck -> Orfeo’s Aria ) 

Religious vocal music

Religious music went into a certain decline, although Masses and Requiems were still composed. One outstanding example is the Requiem in re minor by Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, one of the most important works of sacred music. Mozart’s Requiem is divided into several sections or musical numbers. Some of them are, in turn, divided into smaller parts. Some of these may sound familiar to you from what we studied about Gregorian Chant. We listen to these parts of the Requiem:

  • Introitus.
  • Dies Irae.
  • Confutatis Maledictis.

There is a myth about its composition. The myth of Mozart’s Requiem says that he died while composing it, sensing his own death. However, the reality is that it was a mysterious commission from a count who wanted to pass it off as his own work. Mozart died leaving the Requiem unfinished, and it was his student Franz Xaver Süssmayr who completed it based on Mozart’s sketches, creating the final version. This myth was reinforced by the film Amadeus (1984), where we see Salieri helping Mozart write the Requiem while Mozart is very ill.
